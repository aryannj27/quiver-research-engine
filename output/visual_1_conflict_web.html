The Conflict Web

Interactive network diagram that visualizes the "Pelosi Principle" in one glance — how a single congressional trade connects to committee jurisdiction, corporate lobbying, and dollar flow.

The Insight

A congressional trade is rarely isolated. The real story lives in the web around it.

When Rep. Gottheimer bought $500k+ in MSFT call options on March 25, it was not one data point — it was a node in a web. MSFT was simultaneously spending $170k+ on federal lobbying focused on AI, Section 230, FISA 702, and cybersecurity. Gottheimer sits on House Financial Services. The options had a 3-month expiry window coinciding with pending AI legislation markup.

Retail investors reading the newsletter see the trade. They do not see the web. This visual makes the web legible in 3 seconds.

The Design — How It's Built

Force-directed network graph (D3.js). Nodes sized by dollar magnitude, colored by entity type. Solid edges represent declared relationships (committee membership, lobbying filing, trade disclosure). Dashed red edges indicate the potential conflict path — where jurisdiction, lobbying, and trade converge on the same ticker within a 90-day window.

In production: one Conflict Web per top-ranked lead in the Sunday brief. Each is a PNG export for newsletter embedding, plus an interactive version on the Quiver site that retail subscribers can explore.

The "So What" — if a retail investor sees this at 7 AM Tuesday

They do not need to understand politics or lobbying law. They see a triangle: Congressperson → Committee → Company, with dollars on each edge. They understand instantly: this person had information, this company was spending money to influence that person's committee, and this person put money in that company. That is the entire premise of the newsletter, rendered visually.
